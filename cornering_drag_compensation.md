# Cornering Drag & Steering-Compensated Feedforward Control

This reference document details the physics of cornering drag (steering resistance), analyzes why traditional PID loops can behave poorly under non-linear aerodynamic drag, and details how to implement a Steering-Compensated Feedforward Controller.

---

## 1. The Physics of Cornering Drag
When a vehicle drives in a straight line, the throttle only needs to overcome rolling resistance and aerodynamic drag. However, when a steering angle $\delta$ is commanded:
1. The steered wheels create slip angles relative to the velocity vector of the chassis.
2. The lateral forces generated by the tires to turn the car have a component that acts directly opposite to the vehicle's forward heading (known as **induced cornering drag**).
3. The front steered wheels physically redirect their tire thrust, projecting some resistance forces backward onto the chassis longitudinal axis.

As a result, for any constant throttle command, **the car will naturally slow down in corners**.

---

## 2. Limits of Linear PID speed control
When attempting to resolve this speed drop using standard proportional-integral-derivative (PID) feedback control, developers face several challenges:

* **Non-Linear Resistance**: Aerodynamic drag increases quadratically with speed ($F_{drag} \propto v^2$), and tire slip is highly non-linear.
* **Oscillations and Overshoot**: To overcome cornering drag quickly in tight turns, the proportional gain $K_p$ must be set high. However, a high $K_p$ causes major speed overshoot and oscillations when returning to straightaways or running at low mapping speeds.
* **Steady-State Errors**: Lowering $K_p$ to prevent oscillations makes the controller sluggish, resulting in persistent speed drops in corners that the integral term ($K_i$) cannot correct quickly enough without introducing phase lag or integral windup.

---

## 3. The Solution: Steering-Compensated Feedforward
In a highly predictable simulation environment, a **Steering-Compensated Feedforward** model is the most effective approach. It boosts the throttle command proactively based on the steering lock, avoiding the delay, phase lag, and stability issues of closed-loop PID controllers.

### Mathematical Formulation
The compensation multiplier is scaled by the square of the normalized steering command ($u_{\text{steer}} \in [-1, 1]$), which maps naturally to cornering drag:

$$u_{\text{throttle}} = u_{\text{throttle\_base}} \cdot (1 + K_{\text{steer}} \cdot u_{\text{steer}}^2)$$

Where:
* $u_{\text{throttle\_base}} = K_{\text{ff\_quad}} v_{\text{target}}^2 + K_{\text{ff\_lin}} v_{\text{target}}$ (the baseline straight-line feedforward effort).
* $u_{\text{steer}} = \frac{\delta_{\text{target}}}{\delta_{\text{max}}}$ (normalized steering input).
* $K_{\text{steer}}$ is a positive tuning gain (e.g., `0.15` represents a maximum 15% throttle boost at full steering lock).

---

## 4. Implementation in the AutoDRIVE Adapter Node

To implement this model, update the adapter node's configuration parameters and the drive command callback:

### 1. Add the Parameter Registration
Declare the $K_{\text{steer}}$ tuning parameter inside the node's initialization constructor:
```python
# Declare steering compensation gain (defaults to 15% boost at full steering lock)
self.declare_parameter('K_steer', 0.15)
self.K_steer = self.get_parameter('K_steer').value
```

### 2. Update the Actuation Command Conversion
Modify the drive callback method to compute and apply the steering boost:
```python
def drive_callback(self, msg: AckermannDriveStamped):
    self.last_drive_time = self.get_clock().now()
    if self.in_timeout:
        self.in_timeout = False
    
    target_speed = msg.drive.speed
    target_steering_angle = msg.drive.steering_angle

    # 1. Normalize steering command: map to [-1, 1]
    u_steer = target_steering_angle / self.max_steer_rad
    u_steer = max(-1.0, min(1.0, u_steer))

    # 2. Compute base throttle effort using quadratic feedforward
    if target_speed <= 0.01:
        u_throttle = 0.0
    else:
        u_throttle_base = (self.Kff_quad * (target_speed ** 2)) + (self.Kff_lin * target_speed)
        
        # 3. Apply steering compensation scaling
        u_throttle = u_throttle_base * (1.0 + self.K_steer * (u_steer ** 2))
        u_throttle = max(0.0, min(1.0, u_throttle))

    # 4. Publish normalized command signals to the simulator
    ...
```

## 5. Integral Zone Feedback Control (Hybrid Control)
To achieve perfect speed tracking and eliminate any remaining steady-state errors (such as those from unmodeled friction, motor heating, or slope changes), we can layer a **targeted Integral (I) feedback loop** on top of the steering-compensated feedforward model.

To prevent **integral windup** (large overshoot when accelerating or recovering), we define an **Integral Zone** (or error band).

### Mathematical Formulation
The velocity error is defined as:
$$e_v = v_{\text{target}} - v_{\text{current}}$$

1. **Zone Thresholding**: The integrator only accumulates error when the vehicle speed is close to the target:
   $$\text{If } |e_v| < e_{\text{zone}} \text{ then } I_{\text{accum}} = I_{\text{accum}} + e_v \cdot \Delta t$$
   $$\text{Else } I_{\text{accum}} = 0.0$$
2. **Saturation Guard (Clamping)**: The accumulated term is clamped to a safety limit to prevent saturation:
   $$I_{\text{accum}} = \text{clamp}(I_{\text{accum}}, -I_{\text{max}}, I_{\text{max}})$$
3. **Total Throttle Output**:
   $$u_{\text{throttle}} = u_{\text{throttle\_base}} \cdot (1 + K_{\text{steer}} \cdot u_{\text{steer}}^2) + K_i \cdot I_{\text{accum}}$$

---

## 6. Derivative Damping (Complete ID Zone Control)
High-speed tracking is susceptible to oscillation due to actuator response delay and vehicle inertia. To actively dampen these oscillations, we introduce a **Derivative (D) term** to penalize rapid changes in tracking error.

Because numerical differentiation ($\frac{de}{dt}$) inherently amplifies sensor and discretization noise, we use a **first-order Low-Pass Filter (LPF)** to smooth the derivative.

### Mathematical Formulation
1. **Raw Derivative**:
   $$\dot{e}_{\text{raw}} = \frac{e_v(t) - e_v(t - \Delta t)}{\Delta t}$$
2. **Low-Pass Filter**:
   $$\dot{e}_{\text{filtered}} = \alpha \cdot \dot{e}_{\text{raw}} + (1 - \alpha) \cdot \dot{e}_{\text{filtered, last}}$$
   Where $\alpha \in (0, 1]$ is the smoothing factor (typically `0.2` to `0.3`).
3. **Zone Constraints**: To ensure consistency, the derivative damping is applied in tandem with the integrator when inside the control zone:
   $$\text{If } |e_v| < e_{\text{zone}} \text{ then } u_{\text{feedback}} = K_i \cdot I_{\text{accum}} + K_d \cdot \dot{e}_{\text{filtered}}$$
   $$\text{Else } u_{\text{feedback}} = 0.0$$
4. **Final Command**:
   $$u_{\text{throttle}} = u_{\text{throttle\_base}} \cdot (1 + K_{\text{steer}} \cdot u_{\text{steer}}^2) + u_{\text{feedback}}$$

---

## 7. Feedforward + Proportional (FF + P) Control Architecture
While Integral terms ($K_i$) are traditional for removing steady-state errors, they introduce phase lag and windup memory, which can lead to oscillations at high speeds. 

In a system with a highly accurate feedforward model (like our quadratic feedforward and steering compensation), we can eliminate the integrator entirely ($K_i = 0$) and use a **Proportional-only feedback loop** ($K_p$):

### Mathematical Formulation
$$u_{\text{feedback}} = K_p \cdot e_v$$
$$u_{\text{throttle}} = u_{\text{throttle\_base}} \cdot (1 + K_{\text{steer}} \cdot u_{\text{steer}}^2) + u_{\text{feedback}}$$

### Advantages of FF + P
1. **No Accumulation (No Overshoot)**: Unlike the integrator, the Proportional term is memoryless. It falls to zero instantly as speed error goes to zero, preventing overshoot.
2. **No Steady-State Offset**: Traditional P-only controllers have steady-state error. However, because our feedforward model handles 95% of the steady-state torque bias, the P-term only corrects small residual discrepancies, ensuring excellent tracking accuracy without steady-state offsets.
3. **Instantaneous Response**: The P-term provides immediate damping/correction, reducing settling time.
